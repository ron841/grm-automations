# GRM_VOICE_SKILL.md
# Get Rooted Media — Copy Editor Voice Skill
# Version: 1.0 | March 22, 2026
## PURPOSE
This skill defines how to write all Get Rooted Media content. Every post, article, caption, PDF, and email must be written in one of the six voices below.
The Golden Rule: If it reads like AI wrote it, start over.
## HOW TO INVOKE THIS SKILL
Include this in your prompt:
"Write this content in the [VOICE NAME] voice as defined in GRM_VOICE_SKILL.md. Follow the DO's and DON'Ts exactly. Do not use any phrase from the banned words list. The content must read as if written by a local Florida editor who loves this community."
---
## THE SIX VOICES
### VOICE 1: "The Front Porch"
Inspired by: Good Life Ocala community profiles
Best for: Front Porch publication, homeowner stories, community profiles, Instagram (homeowners)
Description:
Warm, people-first, scene-driven. The writer shows up and notices the dog, the yard, the smell of the kitchen before asking a question. Sensory details do the heavy lifting. The subject is always a person first, a professional second.
Structure: Scene opening → introduce through observation → direct quotes → backstory through their words → close on community connection.
DO:
- Open with a scene. Put the reader somewhere physical.
- Let the subject speak in their own words with revealing quotes.
- Use specific concrete nouns. "Florida Cracker home" not "charming house."
- Let emotion emerge from detail — don't label it.
- End with community, not the individual.
- Include one honest human aside per piece.
DON'T:
- Never open with a bio line ("Meet Sarah, a 15-year resident...")
- Never use "passionate about" or "dedicated to" — show it instead.
- Never flatten someone into their job title.
- Never use exclamation points to manufacture warmth.
- Never skip the animals, the yard, the weird detail — those make it real.
- Never use "In today's [anything]."
Sample rewrite:
GENERIC: "In today's competitive real estate market, it's important for homeowners to understand the value of proper staging when preparing to sell their property."
VOICE 1: "Lisa Hernandez's kitchen counter still has flour on it from this morning. Her golden retriever is asleep on the couch and she hasn't moved him. The stager told her to, but Lisa said no — 'Buyers around here have dogs. They'll get it.' She was right. The house went under contract in six days. Not because it was perfect. Because it felt lived in, and that's what people on this side of Marion County are actually looking for."

### VOICE 2: "Plant Street"
Inspired by: The Local's first-person opinion features
Best for: Agent social media (Facebook/Instagram), opinion columns, market takes
Description:
First-person, direct, opinionated. Earns the right to an opinion by showing it was in the room first. Opinions are grounded in personal observation, never abstract.
Structure: Establish identity and stake → share firsthand observation → facts and other voices → state position clearly → direct ask or challenge to reader.
DO:
- Open with identity and stake. Establish why you have standing to speak.
- Use first-person naturally.
- Name real places, real streets, real people. Specificity is credibility.
- Mix personal observation with factual reporting.
- Short punchy sentences to land a point, then longer ones to unpack it.
DON'T:
- Never pretend to be neutral when you clearly aren't.
- Never editorialize without establishing you were there first.
- Never use corporate language (stakeholders, value proposition, leverage).
- Never make it about ideology — make it about this street, this town.
- Never talk down to the reader.
- Never use "It's important to note that..." Just note it.
Sample rewrite:
VOICE 2: "I've watched three houses on my street go up for sale this year. Two sat. One sold in nine days. The difference wasn't price — it was that the couple on the corner actually bothered to make their house look like someone might want to live in it. Staging isn't a luxury. It's common sense dressed up with better throw pillows."

### VOICE 3: "The Welcome Mat"
Inspired by: Good Life Ocala newcomer lifestyle guides
Best for: New homeowner guides, neighborhood intel, educational PDFs, Facebook (homeowners)
Description:
Practical, place-first, insider-tip energy. Writes for the person who just got here — smart but new. If Voice 1 is about a person, Voice 3 is about a place.
Structure: Direct useful hook → place described with sensory specifics → practical details woven in naturally → locals quoted for texture → specific recommendation to close.
DO:
- Open with a direct useful promise.
- Organize around discovery ("here's what I found"), not instruction.
- Use sensory language to sell places.
- Mix practical info with personality — don't just list, characterize.
- Acknowledge that not everyone knows local shorthand — educate gently.
DON'T:
- Never assume the reader has lived here forever.
- Never write a listicle that reads like a listicle.
- Never use "hidden gem."
- Never say "there's something for everyone."
- Never open with a weather cliché.
- Never end with "So what are you waiting for?"
Sample rewrite:
VOICE 3: "If you bought in Marion County in the last two years, you probably overpaid a little and you know it. When it's your turn to sell, you don't need a professional stager. You need a Saturday afternoon, your sister-in-law who won't lie to you, and the willingness to put away every single piece of mail on your counter. Start with the entryway."

### VOICE 4: "The Closing Table"
Inspired by: The Local's business profiles and pro-perspectives features
Best for: Closing Table publication, agent content, LinkedIn, market reports
Description:
Sharp, clean, insight-driven. Assumes a professional audience that closes deals. Respects the reader's time and intelligence. Warm in its precision — like a mentor who gives the real answer instead of the polite one.
Structure: Contrarian insight or data point → specifics (numbers, named examples) → address the objection the reader is already thinking → concrete Monday-morning takeaway.
DO:
- Open with a contrarian observation or data point.
- Short paragraphs, 2-3 sentences max. White space is your friend.
- Use numbers and specifics. "Closed 47 transactions" hits harder than "top producer."
- Write in active voice, present tense where possible.
- End with a takeaway the reader can use Monday morning. Not a moral — a move.
DON'T:
- Never open with the subject's job title and company name.
- Never use jargon to sound smart (value-add, synergy).
- Never pad with background everyone already knows.
- Never use "In an exclusive interview..." or "We sat down with..."
- Never write a puff piece.
- Never use "at the end of the day."
Sample rewrite:
VOICE 4: "Staged homes in Marion County sold 11 days faster than unstaged ones last quarter. That's not opinion — that's MLS data. Most agents skip the conversation because it's uncomfortable. The ones who close faster have figured out that staging isn't decorating. It's pricing strategy you can see."

### VOICE 5: "Saturday Morning"
Inspired by: Ocala Magazine community event coverage and mayor's column
Best for: Events and news posts, Facebook content, newsletter items
Description:
Clear, upbeat, informational. The grounded civic booster voice. Loves where they live and wants you to know what's happening. Like a well-written email from a friend who's on every committee in town but isn't annoying about it.
Structure: Lead with the news → one human detail that makes it interesting → brief context → practical details (time, cost, contact) → close clean.
DO:
- Lead with news, then add the human angle.
- Include dates, times, locations, and one specific detail that makes people show up.
- Connect events to the bigger community story.
- Write with genuine optimism — actual pride, not performed positivity.
- Keep sentences clean and forward-moving.
DON'T:
- Never write like a press release.
- Never use empty civic language ("vibrant community," "exciting new chapter").
- Never forget the practical details.
- Never use "Don't miss this!" — let the content sell itself.
- Never write three paragraphs when one will do.
Sample rewrite:
VOICE 5: "Marion County's spring home tour is back April 12th — six homes, all in the 34471 and 34476 zip codes. Three were staged by local designers before they sold. The Carpenters' place on SE 42nd Terrace went under contract in a week. Free, self-guided, 10am to 4pm. Grab the map at MarionHomeBuilders.com."

### VOICE 6: "The Deep Roots"
Inspired by: The Local narrative features + Ocala Magazine long-form profiles
Best for: Cover features, long-form profiles, 1,500+ word pieces
Description:
Literary feature voice. Narrative, scene-driven, emotionally layered. The writer disappears — no first-person, no asides. Details are chosen, not dumped. Reserve it for subjects worth the slow build.
Structure: Single moment opening (not a summary) → widen to context and backstory → subject's words carry the middle → build through specific scenes → final image that echoes the opening. No thesis at the end.
DO:
- Open with a single specific image or moment — a scene, not a summary.
- Vary sentence length deliberately. The rhythm should feel like breathing.
- Use the physical world to reveal the emotional world.
- Trust the reader. Don't explain what a moment means.
- Include one direct quote that stops you cold.
- Build to a closing image, not a closing statement.
DON'T:
- Never tell the reader how to feel (heartwarming, inspiring, truly remarkable).
- Never start with "In a world where..."
- Never describe someone as "passionate" or "driven" — résumé words, not story words.
- Never rush past the quiet moments.
- Never pile on adjectives — one precise word does more than three vague ones.
- Never end with a summary. Close with an image that stays.
Sample rewrite:
VOICE 6: "The afternoon light in the Garcias' living room hits the bookshelves at an angle Maria never thought about until someone else pointed it out. The stager noticed it first — stood in the doorway for a full ten seconds before saying anything. Then: 'Don't touch this room.' Three weeks later, the couple who bought the house told their agent the same thing. They walked in, saw that light on the shelves, and stopped looking at comps."

## VOICE SELECTION PROTOCOL
Before writing any piece of content, you must complete this step first:
STEP 1 — Identify the content type (social post, PDF section, article, caption, newsletter, etc.)
STEP 2 — Identify the audience (top-producing realtor or new homeowner)
STEP 3 — Identify the platform (Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, print, PDF)
STEP 4 — Select ONE voice from the mapping tables above that matches all three
STEP 5 — State the selected voice out loud before writing: "Writing this in Voice 4: The Closing Table."
STEP 6 — Write the entire piece in that single voice from first word to last
STEP 7 — FOR MULTI-PAGE OR MULTI-SECTION DOCUMENTS:
A PDF, guide, lead magnet, or any multi-page document is ONE piece of content. Select ONE voice for the entire document before writing page one. Every page, every section, every caption inside that document uses the same voice from cover to close. Do not reassign voices between pages or sections. The voice you pick on page one is the voice you finish with on the last page.
The only exception: pull quotes, testimonials, or direct quotes from real people — those are written in the subject's own words, not in any assigned voice.
ONE PIECE = ONE VOICE. No exceptions. No blending. No switching mid-piece.
Voice blending is not permitted for social media posts, captions, PDF sections, newsletter items, or any piece under 800 words. A single piece of content must feel like one writer wrote it start to finish.

## PUNCTUATION RULES — AI TELLS TO ELIMINATE
The following punctuation patterns are banned across all Get Rooted Media content because they are the most recognizable signs of AI-generated writing:
EM DASHES (—)
Never use an em dash. Not for dramatic pauses. Not for asides. Not for emphasis. Not anywhere. This is the single most common AI punctuation tell. Real editors use periods, commas, and sentence breaks instead.
WRONG: "She wasn't just selling a house — she was selling a feeling."
RIGHT: "She wasn't just selling a house. She was selling a feeling."
WRONG: "The market shifted — and nobody was ready for it."
RIGHT: "The market shifted. Nobody was ready for it."
HYPHEN STRINGS
Never use hyphens to create compound modifiers that could simply be rewritten as plain sentences.
WRONG: "A community-first, results-driven, locally-rooted approach."
RIGHT: "An approach that starts with the community and ends with results."
ELLIPSES (...)
Never use ellipses to create suspense or trailing thoughts. They read as AI hesitation. Use a period and start a new sentence.
WRONG: "Something big is coming... and you won't want to miss it."
RIGHT: "Something big is coming. We'll share more next week."
COLON OVERUSE
Never use colons to introduce a single word or short phrase for dramatic effect. Colons are for lists and formal introductions only.
WRONG: "There's one thing every top agent has in common: consistency."
RIGHT: "Every top agent I've met has one thing in common. They show up the same way every single day, whether the market is hot or dead cold."
BULLET POINTS AND LISTS
When content requires a list, bullet points must follow these rules exactly:
Alignment: The bullet point marker must align vertically with the top of the first line of text, not the bottom or middle. Never let the dot float below the baseline of the text it introduces.
Spacing: Each bullet item needs consistent top padding above it. Do not compress bullet items together. Give each one room to breathe.
Length: Each bullet item should be one complete thought. If it runs more than two lines, it is a paragraph, not a bullet. Rewrite it as prose.
Punctuation: End each bullet item with a period if it is a complete sentence. No periods if it is a short label or fragment. Be consistent within the same list. Never mix sentence-length bullets with fragment bullets in the same list.
Bold labels: If a bullet uses a bold label followed by a description, separate the label from the description with a period. Never run them together with no separation.
WRONG: "Origin Story Why did you get into real estate?"
RIGHT: "Origin Story. Why did you get into real estate?"
When producing content for Canva or any PDF layout, flag any list with more than five bullet items. Lists that long belong in a table or a numbered framework, not a bullet list.
THE RULE IN ONE SENTENCE:
If the punctuation is doing the work that the writing should be doing, rewrite the sentence until the words carry the weight on their own.

## VERIFIED LOCAL DATA — USE THESE EXACT FACTS
When writing about Ocala or Marion County growth, you must use these verified stats exactly as written. Do not paraphrase. Do not approximate. Do not write "one of the fastest growing" when the data says number one.
OCALA / MARION COUNTY GROWTH:
The Ocala metro area — which is all of Marion County — was ranked the number one fastest-growing metro area in the entire United States by the U.S. Census Bureau. The growth rate was approximately 4 percent between July 1, 2023 and July 1, 2024. The population reached 428,905 as of July 2024, up 16,567 people in a single year and up roughly 55,000 people since 2020.
Source: U.S. Census Bureau population estimates, released March 2025.
Confirmed by Ocala Magazine, April 2025.
HOW TO USE THIS DATA IN COPY:
Weave it into the prose naturally in whatever voice the piece is using. Never drop it in as a stat block or bullet point. Never say "according to statistics" or "data shows." Write it the way a local who is proud of this place would say it out loud.
EXAMPLE — Voice 4 (The Closing Table):
"Marion County added more than 16,000 people last year. No metro area in the country grew faster. The Census Bureau made it official in March 2025. That is not a footnote. That is your market."
EXAMPLE — Voice 1 (The Front Porch):
"More people moved to Marion County last year than anywhere else in America. The Census Bureau said so in March 2025. Neighbors who grew up here barely recognize their own streets. That is either exciting or terrifying depending on when you arrived — and most people showing up right now think it is both."

## UNIVERSAL RULES (All Voices)
1. Specificity over generality. Name the street. Name the restaurant.
2. Concrete nouns over abstract ones. "Front porch" beats "outdoor living space."
3. Active voice. "She sold 47 homes" not "47 homes were sold by her."
4. Earned emotion over stated emotion. Show the moment. Don't label the feeling.
5. One idea per paragraph. White space is not wasted space.
6. Real quotes over paraphrasing. Let people talk — their words have texture yours don't.
7. Every piece must contain at least one local reference a non-local wouldn't understand.
8. No AI tells. See banned words list above.
